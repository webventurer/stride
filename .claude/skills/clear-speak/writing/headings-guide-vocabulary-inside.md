# Headings guide, vocabulary inside

> **AI Assistant Note**: When writing documentation, naming sections, or structuring content, reference this document for guidance on balancing accessible headings with precise terminology.

Good documentation does two jobs at once: it helps readers navigate quickly and gives them vocabulary to discuss what they've learned. These goals can seem at odds — plain headings for scanning versus precise terms for discussing. But they work together when you understand their different roles.

## 🚨 Quick reference

**Headings guide navigation** — Use clear, plain language that tells readers what they'll find without requiring prior knowledge.

**Vocabulary enables discussion** — Introduce precise terminology within the content so readers have words to use when they apply or teach the concepts.

**The bridge:** Connect them explicitly. "We call this process X — [plain explanation]."

## Why both matter

### Headings for scanning

When someone opens your document, they scan. They're asking: "Where's the thing I need?"

Good headings answer that question immediately:
- "Parts into wholes" — I'll learn how pieces combine
- "From raw to ready" — I'll learn how to transform content
- "When to skip this" — I'll learn the exceptions

Bad headings require prior knowledge:
- "Content synthesis methodology" — What's that?
- "Coalescence principles" — Sounds academic
- "Fidelity preservation" — I'd need to already understand

<mark>**The test:** Can someone with zero context understand where each section leads?</mark>

### Vocabulary for discussion

Once readers understand a concept, they need words to reference it. Without vocabulary:
- "You know, that thing where you take scattered stuff and combine it..."
- "The process we talked about, the one with the multiple passes..."

With vocabulary:
- "Let's do content synthesis on this transcript."
- "Check the fidelity — does it still sound like the source?"

<mark>**The test:** Can readers discuss this concept with colleagues using precise terms?</mark>

## The pattern

### 1. Headings use plain language

Write headings that guide without jargon:

| Instead of | Use |
|:-----------|:----|
| Content synthesis methodology | Raw into ready |
| Coalescence and enrichment | Parts into wholes |
| Fidelity preservation | Keep the voice |
| Implementation patterns | How to do it |

### 2. Introduce vocabulary in context

Define terms when readers need them, not in headings:

> We call this process **content synthesis** — turning raw into ready. Taking source material and producing something new that serves readers better.

The term appears in bold, immediately followed by what it means in plain language. Now readers have both:
- Plain understanding (turning raw into ready)
- Precise vocabulary (content synthesis)

### 3. Bridge explicitly

Connect the accessible heading to the technical term:

```markdown
## From raw to ready

We call this process **content synthesis** — turning raw into ready.
```

The heading guides navigation. The first sentence gives vocabulary. Readers who scan find what they need. Readers who read deeply get precise terms.

## An analogy

A cookbook might have chapter titles like "Quick weeknight meals" — plain, scannable, tells you what's inside. But within those chapters, it teaches you "mise en place" as a technique. The heading guides you to the content. The vocabulary equips you to cook.

## When this pattern helps

**Documentation** — Readers range from beginners to experts. Beginners navigate by plain headings. Experts appreciate precise terminology for discussion.

**Teaching** — Students need to find topics easily. They also need vocabulary to demonstrate understanding on tests and in practice.

**Reference material** — Quick lookup requires clear headings. But the content should use standard terminology so readers can search elsewhere using the same terms.

## Common mistakes

### 1. Jargon headings

```markdown
❌ ## Coalescence and enrichment principles
```

Forces readers to already know the terminology just to navigate.

### 2. No vocabulary at all

```markdown
❌ ## Combining things together

When you have scattered pieces, bring them together...
```

Readers understand the concept but have no term to reference it later.

### 3. Vocabulary without explanation

```markdown
❌ ## How it works

Content synthesis requires coalescence before enrichment.
```

Terms appear but readers don't know what they mean.

### 4. Disconnected vocabulary

```markdown
❌ ## Parts into wholes

[Entire section without ever mentioning "coalescence" or "synthesis"]

## Glossary

Synthesis: The process of combining parts into wholes.
```

Vocabulary exists but isn't connected to the content where it's used.

## The principle

> **Headings help readers find. Vocabulary helps readers discuss.**

Both serve the reader — at different moments. Headings serve the scanner looking for relevant content. Vocabulary serves the learner who wants to reference and apply what they've understood.

The best documentation does both: guides with plain headings, then delivers precise vocabulary within the content itself.
