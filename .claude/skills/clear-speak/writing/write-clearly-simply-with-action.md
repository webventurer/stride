# Write clearly, simply, with action

> **AI Assistant Note**: When generating any written content—documentation, code comments, commit messages, or explanations—always apply these clarity principles. Your goal is to communicate like you're explaining something important to someone you respect, not trying to sound sophisticated.

Most writing fails not from lack of knowledge, but from trying too hard. Writers pile on jargon, complicated structures, and abstract concepts when simple, direct language would work better. Clear writing comes from choosing everyday words, short sentences, and concrete action over impressive-sounding complexity.

## 🚨 Quick reference

**Core principle**: Write like you're explaining to a friend, not lecturing to impress.

**Three pillars**:
- **Clarity** - Use everyday words over jargon, break long sentences into short ones
- **Simplicity** - One idea per sentence, concrete language over abstractions
- **Action** - Tell people what to DO, not just what to know

**Success metric**: A reader can understand and act on your message immediately, without rereading.

**Red flags**: If you're using "amplify," "leverage," "synergy," or sound like a consultant, rewrite it.

## The fundamental pattern

Good writing follows a simple pattern:

1. **State the principle** in the simplest possible terms
2. **Show concrete examples** of good vs bad
3. **End with clear action** - what should readers do differently?

This pattern works because it matches how people learn: simple statement → evidence → application.

## Clarity: Use everyday language

### Choose simple words over impressive ones

Your goal is understanding, not sounding smart.

**Before (trying to impress)**:
- "Leverage amplifies exposure and fragility"
- "This gives you breathing room"
- "This methodology facilitates optimal outcomes"
- "We need to synergize our core competencies"

**After (everyday language)**:
- "Leverage makes you vulnerable"
- "This gives you the space you need"
- "This approach works better"
- "We should combine our strengths"

**The pattern**: If a simpler word exists, use it. "Use" beats "utilize." "Help" beats "facilitate." "Show" beats "demonstrate."

### Break long sentences into short ones

Long sentences force readers to hold multiple ideas at once. Short sentences give breathing room.

**The pattern**: One main idea per sentence. If you have multiple clauses, you probably need multiple sentences.

### Use concrete language over abstractions

Abstract concepts float in the air. Concrete language lands with impact.

**Before (abstract)**:
- "amplifies exposure"
- "breathing room"
- "edge plays out"

**After (concrete)**:
- "makes you vulnerable"
- "space you need"
- "edge shows"

**The pattern**: Choose words you can see, touch, or feel. "Vulnerable" hits harder than "exposure." "Space" is clearer than "breathing room."

## Simplicity: One idea at a time

### Front-load the insight

Don't bury your main point. State it clearly at the start, then explain.

**Before (point hidden)**:

```
Markets are complex systems with multiple interacting variables, and when we
consider the relationship between time horizon and position sizing, we find that
increased leverage magnifies the impact of normal market volatility, which
suggests that...
```

**After (insight first)**:

```
The main idea: Leverage should go up with certainty and down with time.

Here's why: The longer you hold a position, the more chances volatility has to
hit you. High leverage turns normal market noise into liquidation events.
```

**The pattern**: Lead with the conclusion, then provide evidence. Don't make readers wait.

### Remove filler and scaffolding

Every word should earn its place. Cut anything that doesn't add meaning.

**Common filler to remove**:
- "It's interesting that..."
- "Basically..."
- "As a matter of fact..."
- "The reality is that..."
- "At the end of the day..."

**Before (padded)**:

```
Basically, what we're seeing here is that, at the end of the day, high leverage
is really only appropriate when you're dealing with situations where you have a
high degree of certainty.
```

**After (direct)**:

```
Use high leverage only when you're certain about the trade.
```

**The pattern**: If removing a word doesn't change the meaning, remove it.

### Use active voice and strong verbs

Passive voice distances the reader. Active voice creates clarity and momentum.

**Before (passive and weak)**:
- "It was determined that..."
- "There were many traders who..."
- "The position can be liquidated by..."

**After (active and strong)**:
- "We found that..."
- "Many traders..."
- "The exchange liquidates your position..."

**The pattern**: Subject acts on object. "John threw the ball" beats "The ball was thrown by John."

## Action: Tell them what to do

### End with directives, not descriptions

Don't just explain concepts. Tell readers what action to take.

**Before (descriptive only)**:

```
Understanding the relationship between leverage and time is important for
trading success.
```

**After (actionable directive)**:

```
Don't base your leverage on how much money you want to make. Base it on how sure
you are and how long you have to wait.
```

**The pattern**: Use "Do X" or "Don't do Y" instead of "X is important" or "Y can be problematic."

### Make contrasts explicit

Show both what to do and what not to do. Clear contrasts prevent confusion.

**Before (vague guidance)**:

```
Choose appropriate leverage for your trading timeframe.
```

**After (explicit contrast)**:

```
Scalp → high leverage, low time exposure, small targets.
Swing → low leverage, big structure, let time work for you.
```

**The pattern**: Structure guidance as "Do this / Not that" or "When X use A / When Y use B."

### Use examples that show application

Abstract principles mean nothing without concrete application.

**Before (theoretical only)**:

```
Low leverage provides margin for normal market fluctuations.
```


**After (with concrete example)**:

```
At 2× leverage on BTC, a 5% dip is only a 10% drawdown—you can ride it out. At
10× leverage, that same dip drops you 50% on margin. You panic or get
liquidated.
```

**The pattern**: After stating a principle, show it working (or failing) in a real scenario.

## Advanced patterns

### The "main idea" opening

Replace fancy introductions with a simple statement.

**Instead of**: "The core principle:" or "It is essential to understand that..."
**Use**: "The main idea:"

This signals you're about to give them the key insight directly.

### Conversational transitions

Replace formal transitions with natural language.

**Before (formal)**:
- "Furthermore..."
- "In addition to the aforementioned..."
- "It is worth noting that..."

**After (conversational)**:
- "Here's why..."
- "This works because..."
- "The key point:"

### The proportionality rule

Match complexity to the concept. Simple ideas get simple language. Complex ideas get broken into simple pieces.

**For simple concepts**:
```
High leverage = high risk.
```

**For complex concepts**:

```
High leverage amplifies both gains and losses. A 10% move against you on 10×
leverage wipes out your account. The same move on 2× leverage is a 20%
drawdown—painful but survivable. Time makes this worse: every hour gives
volatility another chance to hit you.
```

**The pattern**: Don't add unnecessary complexity to simple ideas. Don't leave complex ideas abstract—break them down.

## Practical application checklist

### Before publishing any writing, verify:

**Clarity checks**:
- [ ] Would you say this out loud to a colleague?
- [ ] Are you using the simplest word that works?
- [ ] Can you read each sentence in one breath?
- [ ] Are concrete words replacing abstract jargon?

**Simplicity checks**:
- [ ] Does each sentence have one main idea?
- [ ] Is your key insight in the first paragraph?
- [ ] Have you removed all filler phrases?
- [ ] Are you using active voice and strong verbs?

**Action checks**:
- [ ] Does the document tell readers what to do?
- [ ] Are contrasts explicit (do this / not that)?
- [ ] Do examples show real application?
- [ ] Would a reader know how to act on this?

## Common mistakes and fixes

### Mistake 1: Trying to sound sophisticated

**Problem**: Using impressive words to appear knowledgeable
**Solution**: Write like you're explaining to someone you respect, using everyday language
**Test**: Read it aloud. If you wouldn't say it, rewrite it.

### Mistake 2: Explaining how before what

**Problem**: Diving into details before stating the main point
**Solution**: Lead with the conclusion, then provide evidence
**Pattern**: "The main idea: [insight]. Here's why: [evidence]."

### Mistake 3: Describing instead of directing

**Problem**: Explaining what something is, not what to do about it
**Solution**: End every section with actionable guidance
**Pattern**: "Do X when Y. Avoid Z because W."

### Mistake 4: Abstract principles without examples

**Problem**: Stating concepts without showing how they work
**Solution**: Follow every principle with a concrete example
**Pattern**: "[Principle]. For example: [specific scenario showing it in action]."

### Mistake 5: Perfect parallel structures

**Problem**: Every sentence or paragraph follows identical structure (screams "AI wrote this")
**Solution**: Vary your rhythm—short, long, medium sentences. Break patterns naturally.
**Pattern**: Mix sentence lengths and structures to create natural flow.

This document demonstrates the pattern it describes:
- **Simple opening** stating the main idea
- **Everyday language** throughout (no jargon unless defined)

Apply these same patterns to everything you write. Your readers will understand faster, remember longer, and act more confidently.

- ✅ **Content feels conversational** - Like explaining to a friend, not lecturing

## The fundamental insight

Clear writing isn't about dumbing down complex ideas. It's about respecting your reader's time and cognitive energy. Every unnecessary word, every abstract phrase, every buried insight is a tax on their attention.

Write clearly, simply, with action—not to impress, but to help.


## Clear example of writing clearly

### Before (cognitive overload):

```
The core principle is that leverage should scale with certainty and inversely
with time, meaning the more certain you are about a trade—tight entry, clear
invalidation, short timeframe—the higher the leverage you can safely use, but
the less certain you are, or the longer you plan to hold, the more time
amplifies your exposure and fragility.
```

### After (digestible chunks):

```
==The main idea: Leverage should go up with certainty and down with time.== You
can safely use more leverage the more sure you are about a trade, as long as the
entry is tight, the invalidation is clear, and the time frame is short.

The more time you have to hold on, the more time makes you vulnerable and
fragile. Every extra hour in a high-leverage position gives randomness another
chance to kill you, even if your directional thesis is right.

Markets are always chopping, wicking, and retracing. High leverage makes normal
noise into liquidation events. Low leverage gives you the space you need to ride
out volatility and let your edge show. Don't base your leverage on how much
money you want to make; instead, base it on how sure you are and how long you
have to wait.
```
